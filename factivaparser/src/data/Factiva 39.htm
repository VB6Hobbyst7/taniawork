Canada inked a 10-year contract for subsidized vaccine production with a small company called ID Biomedical that was taken over a few times before being absorbed by GSK in 2005. That pharmaceutical giant has also been contracted for the bulk of Canada's seasonal flu vaccines, which it scrambled to

A new set of policies governing the indoor soccer season --which runs from October to March--is being distributed today to players, coaches, parents and others in the soccer league that includes more than 12,000 children and youth, ages six to 18. It will be posted in dressing rooms and referees' rooms.

It follows exactly the policies introduced last week by the Edmonton Minor Soccer Association in light of a possible H1N1 flu pandemic.

"We're still having that idea of 'congratulations, thank you for a great game,' but we're doing it in a different way," said Leinweber.

As well, players are discouraged from sharing water bottles and parents are asked to keep sick players home. Each team must have hand sanitizer available.

Leinweber anticipates some complaints from parents and coaches, but says those who do "may not realize the severity we're possibly facing. We're being proactive. We don't want to ruin the game, but we want to ensure that the health and welfare of players is being taken care of as best it can.

He hopes the policies are temporary. "In fact, we hope we're over-reacting, that it does not turn out to be a pandemic."

While Hockey Calgary still allows its 14,000 players to shake hands post-game, it's following a set of H1N1 guidelines established by Hockey Canada's chief medical officer. Beyond staying healthy, reporting illness and keeping sick kids home from the rink, the guidelines urge players to carry their own hand sanitizer and, where possible, wash their hands with soap and water after handling hockey equipment.

"Without trying to be facetious, the bacteria that's in hockey equipment on a regularly basis dwarfs H1N1 probability," said president Perry Cavanagh.

Players ages five to 21 are cautioned not to touch their mouths, noses or eyes in the hockey arena, said Cavanagh. Players, coaches and refs alike must drink from their own labelled water bottles. Personal items and towels to soak up sweat and are banned from the bench.

Handshaking isn't the only ritual being eliminated from public life. Nevertheless, last Wednesday, just as reports of the disease began to decline, public interest was falling away and pre-summer temperatures were soaring, Australia began its biggest ever vaccination program to combat the flu.

Health authorities fear that in an increasingly borderless world the flu might take hold in the northern winter and spark a resurgence across Australia.

Australia's chief medical officer, Jim Bishop, says Australia's warm summer sunshine, while a proven disinfectant, won't change the nature of a clearly dangerous disease which will remain a threat for the foreseeable future.

The federal government is urging every Australian over the age of nine to get a jab for swine flu, buying 21 million doses of the H1N1 vaccine to ensure there's enough to go round.

Health Minister Nicola Roxon said the government would have sufficient doses for all adults to have the vaccination.

"It's important for those in the risk groups and who are most vulnerable to do so with more haste," Roxon said.

"We are also trying to provide protection across the community so that if this disease has a tail beyond the flu season, if it comes back for the next flu season, that we have enough ... a high enough proportion of our population vaccinated that the spread within the community will be slowed."

The upside of the flu was the creation of a mini-investment boom in health as government coffers opened to those trying to come up with new ways of fighting deadly disease.

The elderly also found that while they're not immune from the threat, a lifetime of building up resistance to infections seems to have given them an edge.

Australia also learned some valuable lessons about what turned out to be a slow and, at times, inflexible bureaucracy, quickly recognizing the need for some sharpening up if we are to deal properly with a more serious outbreak.

A national containment strategy didn't work well, with up to 1,800 cruise ship passengers able to go home despite a couple of fellow passengers being diagnosed with the disease earlier in the year.

The nation's general practitioners, who were at the frontline in the fight against the disease (those afflicted usually turned up first at the family doctor), were also left out of the loop.

Basic supplies of surgical masks, gowns and gloves for the local doctor were scarce.

This week's vaccinations have tested public acceptance of inoculations as parents are advised not to allow their under tens to accept the needle until a more acceptable inoculation for the young is available, hopefully before the end of the year.

There are also groups raising general concerns about vaccination, particularly on websites, alleging the swine flu vaccination has not been subjected to enough research and testing.

But the majority of Australians appear more than willing to roll their sleeves up and accept a jab, trusting science to help keep at bay what could still be a lethal threat.

Most American workers say they have not had any direction from their employers regarding the upcoming flu season and continue to feel pressure to show up for work when sick because of the current economic environment, a new survey found.
