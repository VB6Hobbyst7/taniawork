H1N1 is making the rounds again this fall, and so is the debate over what to call it. Last weekend, 120 agricultural journalists and communications gathered for the annual meeting of the Canadian Farm Writers Federation in Edmonton and heard Alberta communications specialist Lee Funke's take on the matter.

His is a pertinent perspective. Funke was recruited by the pork sector for front-line duty when H1N1 arrived in Canada in April. He was the face and voice of Rocky Mountain House pork producer Arnold Van Ginkel, who was reluctant to appear or speak on camera.

So how does Funke feel about naming the disease?

Non-plussed, at best.

"You're fighting a losing battle trying to get people to stop using (the term) swine flu," he said. Media spokespeople's contact time with reporters is highly limited, and he suggested time be proportioned appropriately.

"Am I going to use all my exposure (to a reporter) to try to get the facts across about H1N1," he asked, "or on what to call it?"

Well, that depends on who you ask.

Some - including U.S. Agriculture Secretary Tom Vilsack, Guelph MP Frank Valeriote and Guelph's Gordon Sloan - argue that explaining why the name is wrong (it's not all about pigs; the flu has human and avian components, too) can indeed lead to a deeper discussion about the disease itself.

From Washington, Vilsack said last week that choosing H1N1 terminology avoids "undue and undeserved harm" to pork producers.

For his part, Valeriote has written CBC accusing the news organization, which has been unmoved by pleas to drop "swine flu," of ignoring its national role.

"Surely there is a greater good to be achieved in the agriculture industry, that both deserves and requires our help, than there is harm to be created, if any at all, by abandoning the swine flu nomenclature," he wrote. "As a public institution, with an identified conscience . . . the public would expect this kind of conscientious response."

And earlier this month, self-motivated agricultural advocate Sloan, who has passionately campaigned against the swine flu moniker in defence of his rural Ontario roots, fired a volley by sending the tri-nation accord on H1N1 to more CBC executives. The accord, signed by agricultural leaders from Canada, the U.S. and Mexico last month, says "identifying new or existing diseases of public health concern with technically incorrect names had resulted in confusion among consumers, market and trade disruptions and significant losses to the agricultural industry."

However, some media stand firm. The Canadian Press commissioned an independent consumer poll last month to test the waters, and the results were stark. The poll showed 90 per cent of Canadians understand they can't get the H1N1 virus from eating pork. Only six per cent of respondents to the poll felt they could contract the flu from handling or eating pork, and just three per cent said they're eating less pork.

The results weakened the anti-swine flu name drive - or did they? As this column was going to press, CBC news online had two headline stories about the disease. One headline was predictable, "Swine flu changes greetings, hockey habits." But the other, "H1N1 vaccine priority groups released," suggests maybe there's some movement after all.

One thing is certain, though - the pork industry considers itself in peril. Ottawa threw it a multi-million-dollar lifeline a few weeks ago, but pig farmers say that hasn't stopped the damage from chronic low prices and high production costs.

At press time, a pork-producer rally was being planned in Mitchell, to address what the organizers label a "crisis."

Deep trouble remains. It's no wonder pig farmers want the term swine flu to disappear.

He lurched out of the gutter, staggered, almost toppled over -- I couldn't tell if he was drunk or simply top-heavy, betrayed by a combination of giant head, curly little tail and gravity.

"Buy me a drink!" he demanded, with an exclamation point.

Ah, there was my answer: Plastered as the Sistine Chapel. Apostrophe had gone over the top, as usual.

"Don't you mean 'Buy me a drink?' with a question mark?" I replied.

I slapped my forehead, winced.

His shoulders slumped. "You forgot."

"Its not like its Christmas," I whined by way of apology.

"It's!" he snapped. "Not its, it's!"

I cringed. Mine was the sort of thoughtlessness that led to poor Apostrophe's downfall in the first place.

You might recall meeting him a few years ago. He was once a proud pillar of the language community, a little possessive perhaps, but beloved by poets who used words like e'er, o'er and 'tis. ("More contractions than a maternity ward," he sniffled.)

Then writers got sloppy, began dropping the apostrophe for no reason, or 'using' it for single quotation marks 'inappropriately.' Even signmakers couldn't get him right: "It's Men's, not Mens," he shouted at the washroom door. It was the absence of an apostrophe at Tim Hortons that finally sent him over the edge, drinking double-doubles.

It's not just the apostrophe, of course. Commas, colons, long dashes -- few seem to understand the common symbols often referred to as the traffic signs of the English language. Text messaging on handheld devices has worsened the confusion; BlackBerry treats punctuation marks as second-class citizens, consigns them to the keypad's cheap seats, stuck in out-of-the-way places accessible only by four-wheel drive and an awkward combination of buttons. The result is no punctuation at all: "cant make it c u l8r"

This is, one must acknowledge, the second sign of the end of civilization as we know it (the first was the sale of lime-flavoured beer). Start breaking the language laws, and next thing you know it's full-scale anarchy, panic-stricken English teachers barricaded inside empty schools while illiterate Road Warrior savages rat race down bombed-out streets with screaming librarians lashed to their fenders.

Which brings us to National Punctuation Day this Thursday. It's a newish holiday, founded in 2004 by American Jeff Rubin, who seems determined to root out the rot. "There's an epidemic of poor punctuation in the United States, much like the swine flu," Rubin says in a press release. "It's too bad there's no vaccine to prevent it.

"Casual shortcuts bred by e-mailing and text messaging have no place in school papers or professional business writing," Rubin writes. "In the business world, words have power and help decision-makers form impressions immediately. Careless punctuation mistakes cost time, money, and productivity." His website -- nationalpunctuationday.com -- points to a 2006 Globe and Mail story about a misplaced contract comma that exposed Rogers Communications to a $2.13-million utility-pole bill.

Rubin is described as a former newspaperman. I'm not sure those are the best credentials for a language campaigner; it's like saying your appendix is to be removed by a former first-aid attendant. Journalists tend to use English in the same way a monkey drives a Ferrari.

Personally, if I had understood the difference between "You never call me anymore" and "You, never call me anymore" I might have avoided a nasty restraining order.

But I get Rubin's point, and so do others. Britain's Lynn Truss surprised herself and the world when her book Eats, Shoots and Leaves: The Zero Tolerance Approach to Punctuation became an international bestseller in 2004. (She dedicated it to "the memory of the striking Bolshevik printers of St. Petersburg who, in 1905, demanded to be paid the same rate for punctuation marks as for letters, and thereby directly precipitated the first Russian Revolution.") Some editions even come with a Punctuation Repair Kit at the front, peel-off stickers printed with commas, periods and semi-colons that can be applied to public signs by guerrilla grammarians.

They can start with the sign on the Mens room.

Commodity prices bounce up and down all the time, usually related to factors aligned with supply and demand. But it doesn't matter which commodity you are following, it seems that every so often, a wacky equivalent of the rubber-chicken effect creeps into the equation to throw everything out of kilter.

Predicting what the markets will do next has historically been about as easy as forecasting the weather.

But Alex McCalla, the U.S. economist who delivered the Daryl Kraft Memorial Lecture last winter at the University of Manitoba, said the "what next?" question is even harder for market analysts to answer. "Globalization with closer and faster linkages between real and financial markets worldwide means agricultural commodity markets exist in a complex maze of inter-related and often conflicting forces," he said.

He was exploring the rapid -- and unforeseen -- run-up in grain and oilseed prices over the past three years, which at their peak caused food riots in 60 or so poor countries. The short-term political instability and rising concern over the world's ability to feed itself were putting more pressure on pundits to predict market trends -- at the very time as it was becoming less possible to do.

No one predicted the rapid swing of capital into commodity markets as fund investors fled the sub-prime mortgage fiasco or the effect the rising consumption of corn for ethanol-based biofuels would have on global markets. A relatively small -- in global production terms -- adjustment in the supply-demand balance coupled with freakishly large injection of volatility, caused by non-traditional market investors that move en masse, was enough to throw the marketplace into a tailspin.

McCalla said agricultural commodities can be grouped into three general categories -- livestock and livestock products, annual field crops such as grains and oilseeds and perennial crops such as palm oil, coffee and fruits.

But within those categories, the markets differ by what share of the global production actually enters world trade, and whether the major producers are also the major traders. They are also influenced by the degree of concentration among major exporters and importers and the types of trade and production policies those players operate within.

All of these markets behave differently, yet are all linked, either because one commodity can be substituted for another or as in the case of livestock, various feed grains are needed as production rises and falls. Q: Should I wear a mask?

A: In general, the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention does not recommend use of face masks or respirators in nonhealth care settings. But there are exceptions.

Because the strain of H1N1 influenza has led to a pandemic, people who are ill with the virus should consider wearing a mask around other people, if tolerable.

People who are at risk for severe illness from the flu should wear a mask or respirator if they are caring for someone with the flu, and should consider wearing one if there is an outbreak of flu in the community.

People who are not at high risk for flu complications could consider wearing one if there is an outbreak in the community, but it's not recommended.

Q: What kind is most effective?

A: There are two types of protection -- face masks and respirators.

Masks are loose-fitting and designed to stop the spread of droplets from the person wearing the mask, not to protect the wearer from viruses.

Respirators are close-fitting and more expensive. They are designed to reduce exposure to airborne particles.

The CDC recommends N95 respirators for health-care workers. The Food and Drug Administration in the U.S. has approved four models of respirators to help reduce exposure during a flu pandemic: 3M respirators models 8612F and 8670F, and Pasture Pharma respirator models F550G and A520G. Q: But do they really work?

A: It's still unclear how influenza is spread, whether through direct contact, the spray of droplets from an infected person to an uninfected person (within a proximity of two metres), or through inhaling smaller, airborne particles.

However, studies show that fit-tested N95 respirators filter out 95 to 99 per cent of influenza particles, and have maximum effectiveness when fitted properly to the face.

A study published in 2008 in the journal PLoS One found that respirators are more efficient than masks in reducing the transmission of respiratory infections.

Q: Is there a correct way to wear a mask?

Make sure it fits tightly and that no hair, clothing or jewellery comes between the mask and your face.

What is a stalking horse bid?

A stalking horse bid is an initial proposal to buy an asset from a distressed company (most often one that is bankrupt or in bankruptcy protection). Usually, the company or its receiver will select a buyer willing to make an offer. The idea is to establish a minimum price for the asset, by getting a first bid out in the open. This keeps lowball offers off the table and sets up a competitive bidding process. The stalking horse bidder usually gets a break fee if it doesn't end up as the eventual winner.

Where does the term come from?

A stalking horse was originally a horse, or something that looked like a horse, that was set out across a field by hunters. The hunters would hide behind it, so that whatever they were hunting would be less likely to get spooked. It has evolved to mean anything that is put forward or proposed anonymously, or through a third party, to test the waters or mask the real plan. The term stalking horse originated way back in the 16th century. The idea of a stalking horse bid is a lot more recent. It is called that because it is a way to draw other bidders out of the bushes.

Where has the process been used recently?

There was talk of using a stalking horse bid to set up a sale of some of General Motors' assets when the car maker was in bankruptcy protection last spring. Recently, the stalking horse bidder for Nortel Network's enterprise business, Avaya Inc., won the final auction for those assets. Richard Blackwell

Q: How do I know if it's the novel H1N1 strain?

A: Unless your doctor orders a test, you won't. That test, which involves a swab of nasal secretions, isn't routinely conducted. Most likely, if your doctor thinks you have the flu, you will be sent home with advice on care.

In some cases, however, doctors will want a more precise diagnosis, which helps inform public health officials about outbreaks. A flu test is also sometimes given to people at risk of becoming very sick, such as hospitalized patients, health-care workers, infants and those with underlying health conditions.

In general, it's not necessary for you to know whether your flu is H1N1 or a seasonal strain. They are treated similarly and have similar effects, but this H1N1 strain seems to be transmitted especially easily among children and young adults.

A: Stay home and rest. You don't want to tax your body when it needs its strength to fight a virus.

Drink plenty of liquids to avoid dehydration.

Don't drink alcohol, because it can increase the risk of dehydration and it weakens the immune system's response.

Don't smoke; that can worsen respiratory symptoms.

Do take over-the-counter pain relievers (but don't give Aspirin to children or teens) for head and muscle aches.
