Public Safety Canada, the agency charged with co-ordinating the federal response to threats such as the H1N1 flu, has yet to complete its emergency plan, the auditor general says.

In the latest of her periodic reports to Parliament, Auditor General Sheila Fraser said yesterday that Public Safety is supposed to ensure that federal departments work together in emergencies.

"The aim is to eliminate the potential for confusion when responding in a crisis and provide a federal point for co-ordination," says the report.

Instead, the department is still drafting the policies and rules that would define what it's supposed to do and how it's supposed to do it.

Public Safety Minister Peter Van Loan said substantial progress has been made in producing a formal emergency plan.

"Our plans continue to be prepared," he said. "We've worked well with the provinces and the territories, our co-ordination has worked well, as we saw this spring with the floods in Manitoba and we will continue to improve and enhance and build our emergency management capacity."

Fraser complained that the cabinet has yet to sign off even on the draft plans that exist.

"We found that Public Safety Canada has not exercised the leadership necessary to co-ordinate emergency management activities, including protection of critical infrastructure."

Separate tests conducted in the United States have shown that children age 6 months to 35 months and 3 to 9 years should have two doses of the H1N1 vaccination, Fauci said.

"Those younger groups didn't have a good immune response eight to 10 days after receiving the first dose, and 21 days after that first dose their response was still suboptimal - 25 per cent for the very young group and 55 per cent for the intermediary group," Fauci told reporters Monday. "However, there was a very sharp increase in immune response after they received the second dose, such that 100 per cent of the younger group and 94 per cent of 3- to 9-year-olds gave a robust immune response eight to 10 days after the second dose. Beyond the anxiety in the country and the blame game in Ottawa over the availability of H1N1 vaccine, there's a fundamental issue of the constitutional division of powers.

It's both simple and subtle. Under Section 91 of the Constitution Act, Ottawa is responsible for "peace, order and good government." It is the central statement of the Constitution, and the ultimate trump of the federal government in running the country.

This means that Ottawa is ultimately responsible for the safety and well-being of Canadian citizens, including those standing in line waiting for flu shots. Except that the lines are run by the provinces.

Ottawa is also responsible for national health and safety standards, approvals for experimental drugs, and the purchase of drugs such as the H1N1 vaccine.

But the provinces, under Section 92 of the Constitution, are responsible for running the health-care systems across the country, including the distribution of vaccine and the determination of priorities.

In other words, Ottawa is responsible for assuring the supply, while the provinces are responsible for meeting the demand.

As the lineups across the country have demonstrated in the last week, this is a work in progress at both levels of government. The manufacturer, GlaxoSmithKline, is making the stuff as fast as it can, but the surge in demand exceeds the supply for the moment. In spite of all its precautions, Ottawa is playing catch-up. There's a lot of pent-up anxiety, and even some incipient panic.

But the media, which have played a role in this, would do well not to underestimate the capacity of Canadians for calm and patience, as long as they see that health-care professionals and providers are doing the best they can under trying circumstances.

For example, Quebec appears to have done a good job in publicizing priorities in at-risk groups and locations where they can be served across the administrative regions of the province.

What's most frightening about this virus is that young, healthy people seem to be most vulnerable. Pregnant women are particularly vulnerable, and are considered high risk. If a pregnant woman gets very ill, both she and her fetus may be in grave danger. Yes, most people who get the flu have a self-limiting illness that resolves within a week.

The H1N1 virus, however, seems to randomly cause severe respiratory distress in healthy people who would not normally be considered high risk. This can be life threatening. Hospitals are re-educating nurses about ventilator operation, and setting up extra hospital areas for patients who will need artificial breathing machines.

In Canada, there are two versions of the H1N1 vaccine available -- with and without adjuvant. This is causing confusion and concern, as most people have never heard of adjuvants. Deriving from the Latin word "adjuvans," which means to help, particularly to reach a goal, an adjuvant is a substance that helps and enhances the pharmacological effect of a drug, or increases the ability of an antigen to stimulate the immune system. Concern has been heightened because pregnant women, who are first in line for the vaccine, have been told that they should preferentially choose the vaccine without adjuvant, as we have less experience with this version.

But none of the adjuvant ingredients have known risks. Pregnant women in Dr. Rossi's family practice are told, especially in the third trimester, to go ahead and get whichever vaccine is available. This is a clear example of the risk of the vaccine being negligible compared to the risk of the illness. Dr. Hailey's pediatric practice has been similarly inundated with telephone calls. Their receptionist tells every caller, "All the doctors in the practice are recommending that everyone get the vaccine."

The fact is that seasonal flu can cause serious complications even in otherwise healthy people -- but just not as often. The yearly influenza vaccine is recommended for people over 65 and for people with chronic medical conditions such as asthma, emphysema, diabetes and multiple sclerosis. It is also recommended for children under two and their caregivers. We both regularly recommend the seasonal influenza vaccine to all healthy patients if they are interested -- because the risk of the vaccine is very low. Seasonal flu is unlikely to be a major problem locally until about January, but it is safe to get both vaccines at the same time. There is no significant interaction between the two, contrary to earlier reports.

What can you do if you don't qualify as a high-risk patient, and have to wait a week or more for the vaccine?

- First, people who are ill should stay at home! Classic symptoms include fever, cough, aching muscles, headache, sore throat, and nausea, although not everyone gets every symptom, especially children. If you or your children are in a high-risk group (again: pregnancy, a chronic health problem, children under two) and you see someone who is clearly unwell -- leave the area as soon as you can.

- Second, wash your hands -- a lot.

- Third, cough or sneeze into your shoulder. If you cough into your hands, and then touch a doorknob, you've left your virus there for the next person.

- Fourth, avoid touching your eyes, nose or mouth -- especially when you are at work or at school.

- Finally, if you think you might have the flu, you have several options. You can visit www.HealthLinkBC.ca[http://www.HealthLinkBC.ca] for more information. You can call 811. Or you can call your doctor, especially if you are high risk.
