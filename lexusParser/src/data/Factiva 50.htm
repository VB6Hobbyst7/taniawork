Ontario is looking at the possibility that morbidly obese people who contract the H1N1 swine-flu virus are more likely to suffer serious complications or death, says the province's top public-health official.

Dr. Arlene King says scientists studying the patterns of transmission of the H1N1 flu virus have identified morbidly obese citizens as a group that might be put atop a priority distribution list for antiviral medication.

Ontario plans to begin a dedicated vaccination program targeting H1N1 this fall.

Initial data put obese people on par with pregnant women, people living in remote northern communities, infants and people with pre-existing chronic conditions as groups particularly susceptible to the new strain of influenza.

King, Ontario's chief medical officer of health, said officials are treating the new link between obesity and H1N1 as "an emerging hypothesis" based on data coming out of Ontario's intensive-care units.

"I would say we would call this emerging information that needs to be better studied at this time," she said, adding morbid obesity "isn't classically listed as a risk factor for complications from the flu."

Nineteen people in Ontario have died and 396 have been hospitalized with H1N1, which first appeared in Ontario in late spring. The virus resulted in mild symptoms for the vast majority of Canadians who contracted it, but can cause severe acute respiratory distress.

King did not offer Ontario-specific data to support the theory, but in July U.S. researchers gave the first glimpse into the possible link.

A study looked at 10 severely ill flu patients at a Michigan hospital who had to be put on ventilators. Nine of the 10 were obese, including seven considered severely obese. Three of the patients died.

"What this study suggests is there can be complications associated with this virus infection, especially in severely obese patients," said Dr. Tim Uyeki, a virus expert with the Center for Disease Control.

The province's order for the vaccine was due Friday but King wouldn't reveal how many doses Ontario is seeking from the drug's Canadian manufacturer, though the expectation is that something less than 100 per cent of the population will be covered.

What the heck's going on with this summer, eh?

Even people who weren't socked in the pocket by the recession didn't feel like vacationing much with all this cloudy, cold, rainy weather.

The outbreak of swine flu and the Toronto civic workers' strike just put the icing on the cake of what is turning out to be a summer to remember - and not in a good way.

But, just as every cloud has a silver lining, there's an upside to this bummer of a summer. In the spirit of the "glass half full," here are 10 good things to celebrate this unsummery summer:

1. Gorgeous Gardens. The grass looks great, the flowers are blooming and lasting for weeks. The city is as green as can be. This cool weather has been kind to lettuce, peas, herbs, carrots, turnips, beets, leafy greens and radishes, says community gardening advocate Susan Berman. She recommends placing a half-filled beer bottle on its side to catch the slugs proliferating in the cool temperatures. "They slither in and they can't get out," so you get to enjoy your crops.

2. No Smog. We've been able to breathe easier this summer as the air quality index hovers in the "very good," "good" and "moderate" range most days. Christine Hampson, president of the Canadian Asthma Society, points out that people whose asthma is triggered by pollution are having a good summer. Fewer particulates in the air, asthma educator Meridene Haynes adds, means less "coughing, wheezing, tightness in the chest and shortness of breath," which are the symptoms of an asthma attack.

3. Last-Minute Plans. If you're a procrastinator, you are in luck this summer: it's okay to do things at the last minute. Reservations at Ontario's provincial parks are down this year, as is daily attendance at facilities such as Wasaga Beach Provincial Park. While the 5 per cent drop is nowhere near the nightmare of the summer of '92, when attendance fell 22 per cent (it rained every weekend of the summer), parks official Bruce van Staalduinen says there's lots of availability. Check the website ontarioparks.com.

4. Power Saver. Who needs air conditioning when all you have to do is open the window to cool down? Toronto Hydro says the average homeowner has saved $22 this year, over last year, in reduced electricity demands. While malls and offices may have kept the air conditioning going full blast, other commercial establishments have reduced their power demands as a cost-savings measure in this recession, says Terry Young, spokesman for Ontario's Independent Electricity System Operator. No fear of brownouts this summer.

5. Good Deals. Want a golf game with that hotel room? How about a free breakfast or an activity program for the kids? The hard-hit tourist industry is trying to tantalize visitors with tempting deals. Collingwood's Blue Mountain Resort, for example, is offering a graduated discount that kicks in the longer you stay. You get 25 per cent off the second night and 40 per cent off the third night. Spokesperson Kelly O'Neil says this is the first time the resort has given the discount, which "has proven very popular."

6. Swimsuit Reprieve. We've been cuddled in our fleeces this summer and therefore saved the ignominy of exposing our pale flesh in public. Who wants to go swimming when the air and the water are so cold? So, we haven't had to squeeze ourselves into fashionable bikinis or shorts, which has the added benefit of relaxing our hair-removal regimens. If you do go to the beach, there are lots of spaces and picnic tables available. No squabbling over where to put your blanket.

7. Low UV. Although we can still be exposed to cancer-causing ultraviolet rays on cloudy days, this gloomy summer has given us some added protection. Organ transplant recipients or people with a history of melanoma or lupus-related photosensitivity can avoid the sun better when it is overcast. However, dermatologist Cheryl Rosen says she doesn't "go around wishing for rainy, cloudy days." She likes the sun and outdoor activities and recommends sunscreen, hats and shade as methods of avoiding UV radiation.

8. Fewer Traffic Tragedies. Whether all those "staycations" reduced traffic on our highways or not, there have been fewer fatalities. The OPP reports that 153 people have died on the highways this year, down from 170 at this time last year. Most notable was a 50 per cent reduction in alcohol-related deaths. OPP Insp. David Ross says he doesn't think the cooler weather has kept drinking and driving down; he credits heightened police presence on the highways.

9. Stink-Free. Although those living near the large temporary garbage drop-offs certainly got a nose full of smelly garbage, the rest of us really didn't find it too bad during the recent Toronto civic workers strike. Had there been a heat wave, who would not have been offended by that green bin odour wafting from the basement or back shed? Even without a garbage strike, the city usually stinks at this time of year due to pollution, inversions, smog and wind flow from the sewage treatment plants. So far, Toronto has been smelling like a rose.

10. Bragging Rights. Did your child get sent home from a camp decimated by swine flu? Did your mother-in-law move in for a month to take care of the kids while your daycare was on strike? Did it rain every day of your holiday? Were you so broke your vacation was a trip to McDonald's? Have you not gone swimming once yet? Did you wear a ski jacket to a picnic? Bring it in, people: we've got the best conversation starter of all time - how I spent the summer of 2009.

No one was injured.

The fire is the latest -- and the most devastating--blow to the colony's livelihood, following years of low hog prices, this spring's drought and the panic brought on by the H1N1 outbreak. Fire officials say the fire struck at about 6:30 p. m. Friday, and it was likely sparked by mechanical parts in the feed mill inside the barn.

The barn would cost $10 million to rebuild, Tschetter estimates.

Ironically, the steel-and-concrete building was labelled "fireproof" by colony members. It is one of the reasons why the colony didn't have insurance on the barn.

"The last three years hammered us to our knees," Tschetter said, adding low hog prices have already pushed the operation into the red. "We try to cut corners to keep things going."

The building contained seven tonnes of feed, which likely caught fire, said Frank Tschetter, Johnny's father and the colony's financial secretary.

"(The cause) could have been a mechanical belt that slipped too much," Frank Tschetter said at the scene of the fire Saturday, still visibly shaken. "The electrical (system) wasn't the problem."

The fire drew crews from Cluny, Gleichen, Strathmore, Dalum and the Siksika First Nation, but it was still not enough to save the building from burning to a total loss.

"By the time (Johnny) got here, he couldn't walk in anymore," Frank Tschetter said. "Chances are, even with the fire department right here, it would have been hard to save it."

The loss of the barn was a shock to the entire colony of 79 members, as well as the nearby town of Cluny, some 100 kilometres east of Calgary.

"We're going to sit back for two weeks and think about it," he said. "But we know we're not going to rebuild it. It wouldn't make sense with the financial crisis...the hog industry's done."

Johnny Tschetter said the colony has other forms of income -- some dairy and beef cattle, farmland and other agricultural-related items--but with the dry spring this year already cutting into the harvest volume, the financial future of the colony is murky.

The barn employed roughly 10 per cent of the colony's residents, and Johnny Tschetter added the loss of the animals is especially hard to take.

"You can be pretty attached," he said. "Imagine having your dog and times that by 15,000. Then imagine how you'd feel. You see them every day. You feed them, you play with them. You shouldn't become attached to them, but you do."

In 18 years as the New York Mets' team psychiatrist, Dr. Allan Lans regularly witnessed player insecurity, depression and grief.

But the recent wave of major-leaguers becoming so stressed that they have been assigned to the disabled list has moved Lans, now a professor of psychiatry at Columbia University, to call social anxiety disorder "the swine flu of baseball -- it's flat-out crazy."

In the weeks leading up to the All-Star break, three players -- one of them on two different occasions -- were formally placed on the DL by anxiety diagnoses.

Just as Kansas City pitcher Zack Greinke was sidelined three years ago, Detroit pitcher Dontrelle Willis, St. Louis shortstop Khalil Greene and, most famously, Cincinnati first baseman Joey Votto have missed playing time this season for non-physical issues.

Greene made a second trip to the DL on June 30 after falling into another unsettling batting slump.

Experts remind that debilitating strains of performing in the world of elite competitive sports hardly are new.

Baseball historians recall some paralyzing doubts, after their first taste of the big leagues, expressed by no less than Hall of Famers Willie Mays and Mickey Mantle a half-century ago. But it hardly is clear whether anxiety disorder is spreading, is more widely reported, or is possibly overdiagnosed lately.

"Many, many athletes I see are suffering from anxiety and it's always been there," says Tom Ferraro, a respected New York sports psychologist who has worked with athletes and celebrities for more than 20 years. "The pressure, being on stage and under scrutiny, this is not easy.

"To get a baseball player or a general manager to put a high-level athlete on the disabled list, rest assured, it's not a mild tension."

Lans agreed that "anxiety disorders are very real," and cautions -- as does Ferraro -- that cases must be judged individually and with thorough knowledge of the player's personal history.

There are multiple sources for feeling sad and low -- Votto cited his father's death last August while Greene admitted he was "internalizing" poor performance -- but Lans noted that "feeling depressed is not depression" and that, in many situations involving young ballplayers, "they're suffering from adolescence, and that's not an illness."

Anxiety, after all, "is normally part of any performance," Lans said. Think of the oft-mentioned "butterflies" experienced by athletes and performers in the arts world, even people giving speeches or talking around an office board table.

"It's a regulatory thing, like getting up in front of the class and giving a talk," he says. "You need a certain edge to perform at your highest level, the thing you see with pitchers -- like bullfighters -- going though a ritual. You learn to control it and, once you start performing, it goes away."

Furthermore, baseball players build up a sort of immunity.

As they advance from school teams to minor leagues to the big time -- to the growing crowds and media and competition -- they develop a resistance or sorts.

"But to suddenly develop anxiety disorders (in the majors)," says Lans, "that's an unlikely setting for these to evolve."

Ferraro argues there "are many issues related to depression in athletes. Physical injuries create major depression, or personal loss. Baseball is an abnormal environment. Players are traded, they're benched, or, like Chuck Knoblauch or Steve Sax, they suddenly can't throw. It's a recipe for anxiety and depression."

Lans and Ferraro agree that a relationship of trust with a trained psychiatrist or psychologist is crucial for proper diagnosis and treatment. And that anti-anxiety drugs are, at best, a last resort.

"A professional psychologist does one thing and one thing only," Ferraro says, "and that is give them time to talk. Listen. On a regular basis. Let them ventilate."

To Lans, putting a player on the disabled list "makes no sense at all.

"In the general public, when you see a very depressed person, you don't put him in the hospital. That makes it worse, because then he'll think he's crazy. At all costs, the guy should be kept at work.

"Baseball is a wonderful thing because, when the game starts, the only thing in the world is the game itself. It's a treatment. Why do any of us go to games? Turn off the cellphone and everything else goes away."

When Mays, one-for-his-first-26 major league at-bats in the early 1950s, despondently asked manager Leo Durocher, "Mr. Leo, send me back (to the minors)," Durocher told Mays not to worry, that he would be the New York Giants' centrefielder as long as Durocher was manager, and the healing began.

In a game in which dealing with failure is paramount, Lans says, there must be that understanding that "kids need something more than at-bats."

I cycled past a dead crow the other day and was hit by a powerful wave of nostalgia: Whatever happened to the eco-scares of yesteryear? Shouldn't we all be dead by now?

They used to be as reliable (and as hair-raising) as summer blockbusters at the local cineplex -- horrifying tales of invasive foreign species that were undermining native ecology and, in the worst cases, threatening human life. These alarming forecasts arrived every spring and, as far as I could tell, were largely spread by newspapers and television newscasts.

This year, nothing. Or hardly anything; another sign, perhaps, of the declining influence of print journalism. There is passing reference in a recent Globe and Mail story to a new "killer shrimp" -- the bloody red mysid, a tiny aquatic creature recently spotted in the Great Lakes, that is said to be "ravenous," and to "travel in packs." Alas, Googling the words "killer shrimp" mostly turns up recipes from California seafood joints.

As for dead crows, they were associated with the 1999 discovery of West Nile virus in New York City -- Africa, apparently, being an inexhaustible wellspring of gruesome diseases. The virus was actually spread by mosquitoes, not crows, and it killed hundreds of people in the United States at its peak in 2003.

There were victims in Canada, too, although fatalities have been declining markedly. Indeed, four of five people bitten by infected mosquitoes experience no symptoms at all, a factoid usually relegated to the scare story's fine print. They were the product of a 1957 experiment gone wrong in Brazil, when 26 aggressive African queen bees escaped a research compound, mated with milder European bees, and began a relentless expansion north.
