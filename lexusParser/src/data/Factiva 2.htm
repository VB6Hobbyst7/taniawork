GENDER AND DOMINANT LEG KEY TO KNEE INJURIES

Kicking, pivoting and landing can be important factors in tearing or rupturing the knee's anterior cruciate ligament that helps keep the joint stable. But gender and which leg sustains the injury may be key as well, a study suggests.

Researchers from St. Louis and Santa Monica looked at ACL injuries in dominant versus supporting legs among 93 athletes, 41 male and 52 female. They were a mix of pro, college, high school and youth soccer players who had undergone surgery for a complete ACL tear.

In all athletes, contact and non-contact injuries were evenly distributed between left and right legs.

The vast majority of players (84) preferred kicking with their right leg, while nine kicked with the left. A little more than half of the injuries occurred in the dominant leg.

Even when researchers looked at non-contact ACL injuries only, about half happened on the dominant leg and half on the supporting leg.

But when they were broken down by gender, the numbers changed drastically. Injuries on the dominant leg occurred in 74 per cent of males, but only 32.3 per cent of females.

Female athletes are at higher risk than males in general for ACL injuries.

The authors speculate that anatomical differences as well as unique neuromuscular patterns during kicking or cutting moves may play a role.

The study appears in the British Journal of Sports Medicine.

Franklin Delano Roosevelt was not talking about CT scans, fetal monitors, mammograms and cesarean sections when he uttered the words, "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself," but he could have been.

With the possible exception of security, there is no area in which fear plays a greater role in shaping policy and practice than health care. Sometimes that is a good thing and saves lives, as the H1N1 vaccine did in Ontario last year.

All too often, though, that is not the case. Fear can be a dangerous, even deadly, policy tool. In some cases, society's intolerance to risk in health care is making people sicker. One of the most insidious examples of how fear can lead to policies and practices that, instead of making people safer puts them at greater risk, is birth.

I attended a conference recently in which Eugene De-clercq, from Boston University's School of Public Health, presented evidence that skyrocketing caesarean rates in the U.S. are making birth more dangerous for mothers and babies, leading to increased health problems, even deaths. The research should resonate in Canada and other countries where C-section rates have also increased sharply.

Declercq is not philosophically opposed to C-sections. They are crucial, he says -- mothers and babies die in countries where they are not readily available. "Cesarean sections are not inherently good or bad," he said. "But there is a balance that needs to be struck."

In poor countries where mothers have no access to life-saving cesarean sections, that imbalance can spell death. It is a significant reason why more than 300,000 mothers around the world die in childbirth every year.

But when the balance tips too far the other way -- when there are too many C-sections being performed without good reason -- mothers and babies also face increased risk of death or injury.

According to Declercq's research, when C-sections are performed in low-risk, uncomplicated births, the neonatal death rate is 58 per cent higher than it is for vaginal births. In addition, some of those mothers are more than twice as likely to be rehospitalized in the first 30 days after birth.

In an age in which some doctors and patients believe C-sections should be available on demand, those are sobering figures. Like any medicine, C-sections should be taken in moderation, but our intolerance for risk has created a culture in which they are almost the norm, in some cases.

Other research has concluded that the best outcomes for mothers and babies is when cesarean-section rates remain below 15 per cent. Rates above that seem to do more harm than good. But in the U.S., nearly one-in-three babies is delivered by C-section. Canada's C-section rates have steadily climbed in recent years into the upper 20s.

Despite the evidence that unnecessary C-sections expose mothers and babies to risk, record rates continue. And what is driving that change in practice when it comes to birth?

To a large extent, it is fear, by the medical profession, and by parents, which leads to more testing and monitoring, a greater tendency to induce labour for fear that the baby will be too large or is overdue, and less tolerance for letting birth progress, all of which leads to more doctors advising more patients that a C-section is the only safe option. In many cases, they are right, but in increasing numbers of births in which there no complications, they are seriously wrong.

Birth is just one example of how fear drives policies that do little to improve health -- and may cause harm.

In the U.S., which spends more per person on health care than any country in the world, there are growing concerns that overtesting is creating more problems than it is solving.

Americans who can afford good health care get screened, tested and monitored at a rate largely unheard of in the rest of the world. We in Canada are often put to shame with comparisons about how quickly an American patient can get access to an MRI or CT scan, for example.

It turns out that all those tests and screens are not making our American neighbours healthier. In some cases, they are contributing to making them sicker. Researchers weighing the risks and benefits of high-tech testing and screening are increasingly concluding that the risks outweigh the benefits, that expanding routine cancer screening does not save more lives and may lead to unnecessary and invasive procedures, not to mention exposing patients to unnecessary radiation.

Groups concerned about the danger of the trend, including the American Cancer Society and others, have recently recommended fewer tests for breast cancer, prostate cancer and heart disease.

The health care system will first have to convince the public that less is more when it comes to testing and screening. A study published in the Journal of the American Medical Association in 2004 found that the American public was so enthusiastic about screening, that 73 per cent of those surveyed said they would prefer to receive a "total-body computed tomographic scan" over $1,000 in cash. This enthusiasm, researchers noted, creates an environment ripe for premature testing, "placing the public at risk of overtesting and overtreatment.

Those enthusiastic patients are likely to find a medical establishment in which, despite mounting evidence that it is often the wrong way to go, the mantra that more is better continues to win the day.

In this way, fear leads to extreme risk aversion, which leads to a culture of overtesting, overtreatment and intervention that puts patients at increased risk. It's an unhealthy cycle. And it's time both patients and professionals stepped off.

Health-care reform? Elections in Latin America? The World Baseball Classic? No subject is off limits for the former dictator's meandering thoughts, which are called "Reflections by comrade Fidel," although they would be more accurately titled "Streams of Consciousness" by comrade Fidel.

Mr. Castro's writings are best suited to one of those bullet-point-style columns ( "It is only a matter of time until the Yankee imperialists crumble ... You can't beat a chicken sandwich and a glass of iced tea on a hot day") but he has eschewed such conventions and instead veers from topic to topic in the same Reflection, from the weighty to the banal.

Usually his observations come to a thudding end, although sometimes he employs a sign-off, as with this gem from last year: "I have nothing else to say today." (Admittedly, this is a device I have considered when struggling to find an end to a column.)

He then summed her up this way: "Cristina is a devoted President who likes to work on a full-time basis. However, when she travels to other countries, she protects her right to have a number of hours to adapt and to exercise; and this everyone respects." Yes, she is admired the world over for her commitment to getting in a little time on the stairclimber.

It's not unexpected that Mr. Castro has devoted much of his writing to blasting the United States and its allies ( "Seven Daggers at the Heart of the Americas"), but he also reserves a considerable amount of space for, well, crazy talk.

First he suspected the H1N1 virus was developed by the CIA, then he decided it wasn't but that Mexico deliberately hid its existence until after Barack Obama visited that country in the spring of 2009.

He thought the 2009 World Baseball Classic was rigged to allow the United States to avoid playing the best teams until late in the tournament. (Fidel was oddly fixated on that sporting event, which he portrayed as a clash between Asian industriousness and work ethic, Cuban moral superiority and American sloth and excess.)

"I initially thought ... that the conflict would start at the Korean peninsula, where the second Korean War would break out, and that another war would immediately follow; the one that the United States would impose on Iran. He understands, though, that most people are not paying attention to the impending End of the World:

Computers: Aren't they the best?

"We don't know which will be the effect on the environment of the nuclear weapons that will unavoidably explode in various parts of the world, and that in the least serious variant will happen in abundance," he wrote on June 27. "To advance a hypothesis would be pure science fiction."

I have nothing else to say today.

They are in their pure form -- free of additives, colouring agents and preservatives, they retain most of their original nutrition and colour -- and they were all produced in a research laboratory at the University of British Columbia using a leading-edge dehydration technology that its developers hope will turn the processing of food, food ingredients, and even vaccines and antibiotics on its ear.

Yaghmaee, who has a doctorate in food science, and her boss Tim Durance have been fine-tuning the technology for more than a decade, taking it from the realm of theory to practical science and engineering.

A company formed around the science, EnWave Corp., has sold one dehydrator to a local blueberry producer and is collaborating with Danisco, a leading global producer of food components, on pilot tests for the manufacture of probiotics for yogurt.

Earlier this year, the Canadian Institute of Food Science and Technology recognized En-Wave with an award for "outstanding applied development in the food sector."

Durance, who has been a professor in UBC's food, nutrition and health program since 1987, and holds 14 patents, accepted the award on behalf of EnWave.

The technology is straightforward in concept, involving a microwave generator not unlike the one you've got in your kitchen, connected to a vacuum chamber where the food is rotated in a manner similar to a front-load washing machine in order to ensure even exposure to the microwaves.

The key is the vacuum chamber, which is where Durance and EnWave co-CEO John Mc-Nicol believe their technology has a significant edge over conventional freeze-drying.

"When you put something under vacuum it lowers the boiling point. Just like going up a mountain, water boils at a lower temperature because the pressure is lower," Durance explains during a visit to the company's test lab.

"We reduce the pressure [in the vacuum chamber] until the boiling point of water is about body temperature. The problem with vacuum is that it reduces the transfer of energy -- it doesn't cross a vacuum well, except for radiant energy. So we use microwave as a source of radiant energy.

"So then, you can dry things as fast as you want. You can dry it in minute if you want.

"Practically speaking, we usually end up around 10 or 15 minutes but it's really just as fast as you want to put the energy in."

EnWave calls the process "radiant energy vacuum" or REV.

Rapid dehydration in a vacuum means there's no time for food to spoil: no opportunity for oxidization to turn apple slices brown because there's no oxygen to set it off, no moisture to allow micro-organisms to release enzymes that cause food to decay. "You will never get anything growing on it," Durance said. "You won't get bacteria or mould growing if the water level is low enough. So we dry it to the point where microbial spoilage is not a problem, not an issue.

"Drying is an excellent preservative. Actually, it's the same preservative as freezing which removes the water as ice -- while drying just removes the water."

The difference is that the REV method is faster than freeze-drying by several orders of magnitude.

However, even after the technology proved viable for food, EnWave foundered both as a potential commercial enterprise and as a publicly traded company.

Some of that was a due to a lack of promotional acumen. Some was due to the need for an innovation that would increase the number of ways the technology could be applied, expanding EnWave's potential to market its technology.

Things began to fall into place in 2007 when Durance finally figured out how to tweak the food-drying method to encompass other organic materials -- particularly for medical applications by lowering the temperature at which the biological material can be successfully processed.

At present, vaccines can be preserved for a few months with refrigeration, but En-Wave's tests with its dehydration method suggest that something like a flu shot or an inoculation against polio could remain viable for a longer period of time without being kept cold.

EnWave achieves that result by exposing the pharmaceutical materials to a level of vacuum that lowers the boiling point of the material to a level below that which would be required to freeze it -- essentially freeze-drying without using refrigeration.

It remains to be proven that REV will be cost effective at a mass production level, but the implications are intriguing for efforts to distribute medical aide in the Third World, or quickly vaccinate large numbers of people against an H1N1 flu outbreak.

One of the company's pharmaceutical dehydrators is undergoing tests by the Saskatchewan Research Council, one of Canada's leading agencies for research into the commercialization of technology.

McNicol came aboard in 2007 as the broader possibilities of REV became apparent, bringing a level of entrepreneurial acumen he'd acquired in the successful development of an unrelated drying technology, using paper products, a decade earlier.

Today the company has $6 million in capital, a $175,000 a month cash-burn rate, no debt and a market cap of $60 million.

"The freeze-dry industry has been around for 50 years. It was extremely established," McNicol said in an interview. "When I got in there and looked at how they do it, it was anywhere from 24 to 72 hours of processing time in batches.

"It's old, very slow, and very expensive. I call it a beautiful science but it's an awkward operating method."

EnWave's business model will centre on licensing the technology and collecting royalties from the companies that use it.
