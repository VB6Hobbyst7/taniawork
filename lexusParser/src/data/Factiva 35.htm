As the nation embarks on an unprecedented mass immunization campaign, Canadian doctors consulted by the World Health Organization say shortness of breath is emerging as a hallmark sign of potentially critical H1N1 infection.

Experts have been struggling to understand why what is an uncomplicated illness for most people can be severe or fatal in others, and whether there is any way of predicting who is at risk of becoming critically sick with human swine flu.

Now they believe they have a sign.

"Influenza does a lot of things to a lot of people, but the one thing it should never do to anybody, pretty much, is make you short of breath," said Dr. Anand Kumar, with the Winnipeg Regional Health Authority, who specializes in the care of critically ill patients.

"That's a danger signal for everybody," Kumar said told Canwest News Service. "If you are short of breath, that means you need urgent assessment by a physician or by the emergency room.

"That would allow therapy early enough to hopefully prevent it from becoming a severe case."

Kumar was among a contingent of Canadian doctors who participated in an expert advisory meeting convened by WHO this month to develop updated guidance documents on the clinical picture and management of H1N1 disease.

Those guidelines are expected to be released this week.

Data presented at the meeting suggest that one-half to three-quarters of those who get severe H1N1 are relatively healthy.

"Because the general recommendations are that people with mild disease don't have to be treated, the question then becomes, if you're relatively healthy, when do you seek medical care?" Kumar said. "If you shouldn't treat everybody, when do you start taking anti-virals?"

"I think the (WHO) statement will basically say -- and I think we all feel -- that if you're not improving by the third day, if you haven't plateaued or you're getting worse by day three or four, it's time to go in and see a doctor, and they should consider the possibility of anti-viral therapy."

Symptoms of H1N1 are the same for regular seasonal flu and include fever, cough, sore throat, runny or stuffy nose, body aches, headache, chills and fatigue.

"But those are the obvious ones," Kumar said. "The one that we were most concerned people would overlook is shortness of breath."

In severe cases, people have deteriorated rapidly and gone into respiratory failure with 24 hours.

The first batches of the long-awaited H1N1 vaccine will be released across Canada this week in what will ultimately become the largest immunization campaign in the nation's history. The shots are being rolled out in stages and will concentrate first on those considered at highest risk, including health-care workers, pregnant women, people under 65 with chronic diseases, children ages six months to five years, and individuals, including First Nations people, living in remote or isolated communities.

Interspersed were the frightening discovery of Cryptococcus neoformans on Vancouver Island in 2002, which led me to hold my breath every time we drove past Parksville, and the arrival of SARS in 2003, after which I swore off Toronto forever. (OK, that's a fib: I swore off Toronto in 1978.)

SARS was the one that shook our confidence in pandemics. It was supposed to sweep the globe like Tamagotchis, or the Atkins diet, or the Spice Girls. Luckily, only 800 people died during the outbreak, including 44 in Canada. More Canadians are hit by lightning in an average year than were killed by SARS.

Which brings us to H1N1. It is already much more widespread in Canada than was SARS. As of Friday, about 4,800 people had succumbed to the disease worldwide, including 80 in Canada. But the thing is, the overall death rate from H1N1 isn't much greater than it is for the seasonal flu that leaves you singing to the porcelain now and then -- although H1N1 has a different profile, hitting younger people harder. The B.C. Centre for Disease Control says 4,000 to 8,000 Canadians die of seasonal flu and/or pneumonia every year.

But look at the difference in response: A coughing passenger was asked to leave a Victoria bus the other day. Ottawa churchgoers have been asked to stop holding hands during the Lord's Prayer. In Tokyo, visitors to a large corporate headquarters were directed to gargle with a medicinal substance before entering any room. I am, if necessary, fully prepared to panic. But as a layman, gosh, it's hard to figure out when.

Mostly, just use common sense: Wash your hands. Stay home when sick. Sneeze into your elbow. Keep your fingers out of your nose, or the noses of others. And let your family back inside.

It was just a few months ago that authorities were under intense pressure to acquire an adequate supply of the vaccine before the second, potentially more virulent wave of the H1N1 virus hit this fall. But how big is this swine flu thing compared to other highly infectious pandemic diseases -- tuberculosis, for example?

One Swedish researcher recently examined the numbers of deaths versus media pieces related to swine flu and TB.

In a two-week period, from April 24 to May 6, there were 31 deaths related to swine flu (29 in Mexico and two in the U.S.). In that same period, there were 63,066 deaths from tuberculosis, mostly in the poorer parts of the world.

That two-week period, according to a Google News search, generated 250,000 media stories related to the swine flu compared to 6,500 for TB, making the news-per-death ratio for swine flu 8,176 news reports per death, versus TB's 0.1 story per death.

In other words, every person who died due to swine flu equalled about 8,000 news stories around the world, yet it took nearly 10 TB deaths to generate even one news story.

What is wrong with this picture?

What characterizes the news coverage of H1N1 flu, more than any other feature, is its volume. It is on the front pages, the airwaves, the TV, the Internet, everywhere, which adds to the hysteria and sense of panic over a disease that, comparatively speaking, doesn't seem to be particularly lethal.

TB, on the other hand, is perhaps not a "pandemic," but is found on almost every continent and we even get it in B.C. as it occasionally rears its head in places of poverty.

And while the majority of people infected with the H1N1 virus make a full recovery and don't need medical attention or antiviral drugs, the same can't be said for TB.

Tuberculosis spreads easily through the air and many of us carry latent infections. Some of those with latent TB will eventually become active disease carriers and, if left untreated, it can kill more than half of them. TB is treatable with antibiotics.

According to the WHO, new TB infections occur at a rate of one per second.

Despite this, the disease which infects nearly nine million new people per year barely manages to pierce the front pages of our papers.

Saving lives of people with easily treatable diseases is important. And we must act, to do something against the world's biggest killers, even if it is out of the reaches of the media's spotlight.
