One U.S. congressman has demanded, preposterously, that the Mexico-U.S. border be sealed. That's panic. And Michael O'Leary, head of the Irish discount airline Ryanair, said the outbreak "is a tragedy only for people living ... in slums in Asia or Mexico." That's callousness.

In Canada the public-health response to swine flu seems to have neither of those extremes. Health Minister Leona Aglukkaq has trusted her officials, notably chief public-health officer Dr. David Butler-Jones. She even spoke with Opposition health critics before her Sunday press conference.

Aglukkaq is a rookie in Ottawa but was health minister of Nunavut. After the 2002-03 SARS outbreak she took part in developing a federal-provincial pandemic plan. Her calm vigilance now strikes us as exactly the right approach.

So far the death toll from this new strain of flu, even in Mexico, is not significantly higher than the flu toll in almost any year.

Was Mexico slow in responding to this outbreak? That is to be expected in a poor country. And new diseases rarely emerge from wealthy enclaves - the worst-nourished, least-cared-for populations incubate new contagions the best.

Sealing borders, or even restricting travel, won't help, the World Health Organization notes, because you can be infected but without symptoms for several days.

For governments, as for individuals, this is a time for alert calm caution, and not for over-reaction.

Two years ago, while vacationing in the Mayan Riviera, Matt Lewis and Jodi Threlfall agreed there was no better sandy beach to exchange their wedding vows.

Nearly every day since their engagement 14 months ago has been spent finalizing the details of their destination wedding -- scheduled for next week in Mexico.The photographer is booked, the cake's been ordered and the marriage licence is waiting to be picked up.

But the international spread of the swine flu has led their airline to cancel the flight, leaving the couple scrambling to make new plans.

"It was our dream wedding," said Lewis, 28, adding 40 guests were expected to leave Friday, for the Wednesday nuptials. The travel agency is now trying to find another destination, he said.

"At this point we'd be happy just to find somewhere sunny and hot, and still get married on the day we planned," said Lewis, adding he thinks the airlines are overreacting.

"But we don't want anyone to get sick either, so this is the safest thing to do."

WestJet Airlines and Air Canada joined several carriers in suspending flights to destinations in Mexico due to the flu scare.

"What's most important to us is the safety and well-being of our guests so we have suspended flights until this situation is resolved," said Robert Palmer, a spokesman for WestJet.

WestJet and WestJet Vacations will cease service to Cancun, Cabo San Lucas, Mazatlan, and Puerto Vallarta as of Monday, said Palmer, adding the company is trying to reach customers in Mexico to have them reschedule return trips.

Air Transat, Nolitours and Transat Holidays also suspended all travel to Mexico, while Carnival Cruise Lines cancelled port stops there as the outbreak climbed to more than 150 deaths.

Laurie Keith, honeymoon and destination specialist with Romantic Planet Vacations Inc., says Lewis's wedding is one of three she has booked in the Mayan Riviera for the next 10 days. Guests are scheduled to fly in from all over North America and Europe. Caterers have been booked, cakes ordered, and complex legal requirements have been met, she said. "It's really hard to re-organize all of that to another destination,"Keith said.

Travel agencies continued to be inundated Tuesday with calls from travellers looking to cancel or reschedule vacations over concerns about swine flu after the Canadian government issued an advisory against all non-essential travel to Mexico and several airlines cancelled flights to the sunny destination.

"There's a fear of the unknown and, until the World Health Organization can contain this virus, and verify the breadth of it, people will continue to fear travel to Mexico," said Allison Wallace, spokeswoman for Flight Centre, a travel agent with 130 stores across Canada.

Travel clinics in Calgary reported heavy traffic Tuesday.

The health system's answer to swine flu, it seems, is to keep people away from the health system. Alberta authorities want you to wait it out at home.

That seems like odd advice for handling a new flu outbreak. In a serious pandemic, it could get you dead.

So you have to wonder if the experts think this is a real porcine panic. or just a half-baked Hamageddon.

Here's a hint from Dr. Andre Corriveau, Alberta's new chief medical officer of health: "We recommend anyone with a fever or cough who has returned from a recent trip to Mexico to recover from their illness at home, and call Health-Link Alberta before seeking medical attention."

Yes, people just back from virus central are supposed to self-diagnose before seeking help. Without doubt, the authorities are trying to keep the pressure off the hospitals. But the ad-vice would surely be more urgent in the face of a real crisis. The uproar is already being likened to the great 1976 swine flu panic that never got out of the barn.

The Boar Horsemen of the Aporkalypse rode wild for months, but most U. S. victims were killed by the vaccine ordered by then-president Gerald Ford.

Dr. Corriveau certainly sounds hopeful this new swine flu isn't a powerful scourge--not yet, anyway. Every Canadian case so far has ended in full recovery.

In a long Tuesday of local and national news conferences, our local doc said the most intriguing thing of all.

He noted only 25 deaths in Mexico have been officially linked to swine flu--even though the "official" total, bandied about everywhere, is nearly 150. Corriveau said the extra fatalities may be from other causes, including a serious earlier outbreak of a typeBflu.

It's tough to get a grip on the situation in Mexico because of poor monitoring and testing. That's why Canada sent people down there--to find out what's going on. And one finding, it seems, is that the new flu bug might not be extremely serious even in Mexico.

When we switch over to Dr. Butler Jones, Corriveau's federal counterpart, the story sounds a bit more grim, but not by much. He expects some Canadians will die, as vulnerable people always do from any sort of flu (up to 300 every year in Alberta from routine outbreaks that never cause panic.)

Jones also notes, however, that a new virus always has potential to switch tactics and become deadly. He finds himself "humbled" before the adaptive power of a virus.

Then again, a virus that has just switched from animals to humans is equally likely to fade away.

It's not so easy working with a new host, apparently. In effect, the virus says-- "You're not a pig? Disgusting!"-- and gives up.

You might call this silly journalistic anthropomorphizing. You might be right.

But the public health doctors do the same thing. They talk about how the virus "behaves" or "acts" or "modifies its behaviour."

They seem to see it as a vast swarm with a kind of group intelligence, like an invisible ant colony.

So far, this swarm is acting pretty much like its earlier cousins. It's not to be trifled with--not at all--but it's a long way from Aporkalypse Now.
