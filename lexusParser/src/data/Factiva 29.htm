But the terrorist attack came on American soil, and in the public imagination SARS seemed mostly confined to Toronto and places from which it originated in Asia, an exotic sickness that could be avoided.

The collective shiver over H1N1 suggests the buffer has collapsed completely: lineups mass at clinics; a woman out in B.C. is hustled off a bus because she's coughing; office workers take to all but gargling with hand sanitizer; Anglican churches forbid dipping hand-touched wafers in communal wine (yet incongruously permit all lips sharing the same chalice.)

There are historic moments when a common fear seems to unite most everyone, when public perception is uniformly rocked. The H1N1 scare is one of those moments. As though mobilizing for war, governments urge Canadians to get vaccinated, the 21st-century version of the air raid siren.

"It's a hydra-headed fear," said psychologist Frank Farley at Temple University. "We wonder, how dangerous is the virus on one hand, and also how important is it to get the vaccine? And how likely is the virus, or the shot, to impact our children's lives?"

H1N1 is not on pace to kill nearly as many as seasonal flu. The Public Health Agency of Canada says that "between 2,000 and 8,000 Canadians can die of influenza and its complications annually, depending on the severity of the season."

But H1N1 has killed enough Canadians nationally -- 95 in six months, six this week -- and raised enough uncertainty to pump up the fear volume across all demographics. Meanwhile, the slow delivery of the vaccine, which has contributed to the lineups, has only further inflamed anxiety.

The recent death of a seemingly robust southern Ontario teenage boy from H1N1, and the media coverage of the tragedy, brings it closer to home, creating what psychologists call the "availability heuristic" -- a phenomenon in which people predict the frequency of an event based on how easily an example can be brought to mind.

More important than the number of dead is who is dying, and how. The virus reportedly can kill by turning a strong immune system against itself in a healthy person. In our health-conscious, "worried well" society, where we believe clean living controls our fate, this is the ultimate real-life horror movie scenario.

* * *

Psychologists say fear is simply about perception: the more you feel susceptible to something, the more scary it is to you, even if the emotion is independent of statistical probability. People fear shark attacks, plane crashes, tornadoes, terrorist attacks, yet do not dwell much upon dying from heart disease or a car collision, even though the latter two are far more likely to kill.

Other elements of the equation: we fear things we can't see, things borne of nature ("natural disasters") and illness that seems easily contagious. We have been wired ever since childhood to fear infection, said psychologist Peter Bieling, manager of mood and anxiety services at St. Joseph's Healthcare.

"And arguably every zombie movie ever made is about a pandemic."

Couple the frightening elements of H1N1 with our tendency to turn statistical probability on its head, and the fear mutates. Bieling calls H1N1 a "statistical killer." "We're not very good at doing math like this," he said. "We just get a feeling that this is dangerous."

"A new credibility gap seems to be growing, where people don't trust leaders to tell the truth," she said. "This could also lead to panic if people are afraid but don't feel they can trust science or medicine to provide a solution."

Parents fear doing nothing and their children contracting the virus and dying; or they fear vaccination will produce a side effect that will do harm.

We are motivated to act by what psychologists call sins of commission or omission. University of Western Ontario psychologist Leora Swartzman says most people fear actively doing something that could, in theory, hurt their child (commission) as opposed to doing nothing and harm resulting from it (omission).

"A parent thinks, 'I can't bear to imagine that something bad would happen to my kid because of something I did,' " she said. "But if I don't do anything, and something happens, they can say, it's fate.

Somewhere in there, somewhere between my 12-year-old son's hacking cough and fever -- which may or may not have constituted H1N1 -- and a neighbour child's swine flu ankles hurting so bad she couldn't walk, I became paralysed with fear. Somewhere between trying to remain rational and want-ing to race my family to a bomb shelter every time I saw somebody sneeze, I lost my way as the calm voice of wisdom for my family. Somewhere between Moms-againstmercury.org's "Don't inoculate!" and the U.S. Center for Disease Control's "Do inoculate!" I became a bad mother.

Never mind instincts. The only body part not immobilized by confusion were my hands, flitting across the keyboard as I sought answers to the unanswerable: Should I risk mercury poisoning from the evil, conspiring, capitalistic government-sanctioned vaccination? Or do I risk being labelled an abusive parent by not inoculating my children against the dreaded illness? Should I send runny noses to school, that grubby North American institution where swine flu is germinating faster than teenaged love? Or do I keep moderately sick kids home longer than usual, only to face backed-up homework and stronger H1N1 when they return? Should I believe that even the slightest temperature/cough is H1N1? Or do I assume my kids have H1N1 only if their ankles hurt?

Even my go-to health-care provider was overwhelmed, telling me instead of crowding her office or the emergency room with my son's potential H1N1, "Just assume his flu symptoms are swine flu and please go home and act accordingly."

The mother of the girl with the aching ankles said her pediatrician wouldn't go near her child to test or treat her: The doc has an infant and an immune-compromised four-year-old at home and didn't want to risk infection herself.

Just assume. Act accordingly. Please go home.

How much do we have?

Ottawa has received 83,500 doses of H1N1 vaccine, according to the public health department, and is expecting another shipment of 49,500 doses by today. The federal government ordered millions of doses on behalf of the provinces; the provincial governments take delivery and then ship the vaccine to regional health units according to which areas need it most.

How much have we given out?

Approximately 35,000 doses in the city's vaccination clinics, according to the health department's Friday estimates, and a further 14,000 doses in hospitals.

How long will our current supply last?

Till about next weekend, says the health department.

When are we getting more?

There isn't another scheduled delivery for Ottawa, but the federal and provincial authorities have been making shipments as supplies become available. A week's supply is the most Ottawa's authorities have had in hand since vaccinations began last week, but all provinces have been told to expect the flow to diminish sharply this week.

Why's that?

The Canadian factory run by GlaxoSmithKline and contracted to make the vaccine switched its production over to a slightly different form of the vaccine thought to be more suitable for pregnant women, one without an "adjuvant" booster ingredient. After making 1.8 million doses of that, the factory (near Quebec City) has switched back again, but the conversions created a big dip in supply that's working its way through the system.

How big a dip?

According to Health Canada, 2.1 million doses were sent out across the country this past week. Next week, the forecast is only 660,900 doses, including both adjuvanted and non-adjuvanted versions. Ontario's share is to go from 785,000 doses to 256,800.

Is the non-adjuvanted version better for pregnant women?

Early Friday, the World Health Organization issued a report saying there's no reason for them to favour the non-adjuvanted vaccine, but by that time the GlaxoSmithKline factory had already made the switch. Ottawa Public Health says pregnant women can choose which version they want when both are available. So far, only the adjuvanted version has arrived in Ottawa.

What does the drop in supply mean in Ottawa?

We have enough to keep distributing it at current rates, but don't expect to see more clinics opening or much vaccine distributed to family doctors to give. Before vaccinations started, Ottawa Public Health said it would take a week or two to start giving shots to people outside the priority groups of very young children, pregnant women and people with chronic medical conditions; that likely can't happen before the supply is renewed.

"Our vaccine clinic performance has steadily improved," rising from 4,200 shots Monday to 8,550 Thursday, she said.

For the water problem, the answer is to get more pipes, or one bigger pipe.

This weekend will be a whole new operation, with earlier hours; Etches said Friday the city is expecting about the same numbers of people as on weekdays, but no one knows for sure.

There's one new rule: Clinics open at 9 a.m., and numbered wristbands will be handed out starting at 7:30. People at the front of the line will be told to stick around; those farther back will be given a time when they should return.

T ricia Felber is determined to do whatever she can to protect her two young daughters from the H1N1 flu.

Her family follows a strict regimen of homeopathic remedies, eats plenty of fruits and vegetables, avoids sugar and strives for a proper night's sleep.

Felber will not, however, allow her children to be immunized. She has doubts about the H1N1 vaccine's safety and even whether it can fight the flu at all.

"To me, it's not worth the risk," said the 35-year-old Calgarian.

"We are following a homeopathic flu protocol and I feel completely safe with that. Homeopathy dates back 200 years. How long has this vaccine been out?"

Demand for free H1N1 shots has overwhelmed Calgary's five flu clinics, forcing health officials to turn people away Friday and Albertans to wait until high-risk people are inoculated first.

The province's supply of flu vaccinations is dwindling and Health Minister Ron Liepert said it could run out completely by next week if people continue to flood clinics at the same pace.

But even as thousands of Calgarians scramble for a shot, others are seeking alternatives to try to prevent or treat the illness, whether it's a cocktail of vitamins or over-the-counter homeopathic remedies.

People are arriving at organic food shops in droves in search of everything from oil of oregano to vitamin D3 and beta carotene in an effort to ward off the flu.

"We're seeing an upswing in demand for products designed to help boost the immune system," said Liam Muzichuk of Community Natural Foods.

"If I had to hedge my bets, I would say definitely people are coming in because they don't want a swine flu shot," said Muzichuk.

Federal health officials have repeatedly made the case for inoculation, calling it a choice between a safe and effective preventive vaccine or a very real risk of disease.

Earlier this week, a healthy 13-year-old Ontario died after contracting what seemed like a mild case of the flu.

The vaccine is safe, officials stress. But polls have suggested approximately half of Canadians do not plan to get a shot--a statistic that doesn't surprise Erin Bosch, owner of Xerion Homoeopathie.

She said people are showing up at her clinic "scared to death" about the H1N1 vaccine.

"Most of the deaths we've seen are in weakened people. If you build up your immune system, avoid junk food and drink lots of water, you can fight off a phenomenal number of things," said Bosch.

"We don't give the human body enough credit."

Antonin Kodet is a Calgary naturopathic doctor who considers the decision on whether or not to vaccinate a "highly individual" choice.

But he emphasizes that everyone can reduce their risk of sickness by getting enough sleep and plenty of fresh air.

"People who spend more time outside -- provided it is a clean environment -- develop greater resistance and rarely get sick with flus and upper respiratory infections," said Kodet.

Public concern over vaccines shouldn't be simply dismissed, said Dr. Pieter Nieman, a professor of pediatrics at the University of Calgary.

He recommends that parents take a common-sense approach to this year's flu season. "If you just talk about the vaccine and you don't also talk about nutrition and exercise, you're basically standing on one leg," Nieman said. "It's two sides of the coin."

There was a tradition of suspicion about vaccination in Quebec, a tendency to take smallpox for granted as one of the many diseases sent by God to punish sinners. When the first vaccines used in Montreal turned out to be contaminated, causing cases of erysipelas, fear of the vaccine became greater than fear of smallpox. With vaccination suspended or resisted, the virus spread like wildfire among unvaccinated French-Canadian children, and within a few months caused more than 3,000 of them to die horribly, unnecessarily. Enlightened and vaccinated observers were appalled that fear, ignorance and fatalism had combined to trump common sense and good medical judgment. There had been rioting in Montreal's streets when the Board of Health tried to make vaccination compulsory.

After this disaster, the last major epidemic of smallpox in a major city, serious enforcement of compulsory vaccination, supported by such contagion-control measures as closing of schools and churches, became common resorts in the 20th century's mopping up of smallpox in every advanced country (though the phrase conscientious objector was coined in England to describe certain anti-vaccinationists). That campaign ended triumphantly in the 1970s, when smallpox disappeared. The last Canadian case was in 1962, the last case anywhere in the world was in Somalia in 1977.

If smallpox were to appear in any country today, there would be immediate resort to widespread and compulsory vaccination, along with compulsory enforcement of isolation and quarantine. Similarly draconian measures were surely being contemplated by public-health officials in Canada in 2003, when it briefly seemed that the powerful SARS virus had the potential to rage out of control.

THE DEFEAT OF SCOURGES

Louis Pasteur called his procedure to immunize against rabies vaccination in homage to Jenner, and the term now is used to cover most immunization processes. Over time, researchers developed vaccines against other horrible scourges the defeat of polio by the Salk and Sabine vaccines since the 1950s has been particularly notable and even against diseases that had previously been considered mild rites of childhood, including measles, mumps and chicken pox. In the last few years, vaccines have become increasingly the weapons of choice against the constantly mutating influenza virus. Some influenza strains are more dangerous than others. In 1918 there was no vaccine to protect against a particularly serious form of the virus, and it killed tens of millions. Helpless populations could only try to reduce contagion by wearing masks and staying away from possible contacts.

Even as vaccination has become more widely prescribed, safer and generally accepted, anti-vaccinationism has persisted as a minor but tenacious challenge to the medical consensus. As well, the fear-risk-benefit calculations we have to make in getting vaccinated change slightly with each disease. Few sensible people would not accept vaccination against smallpox, if it became necessary, or against polio. There has been significant resistance to measles vaccine (Quebec is still the centre of Canadian anti-vaccination sentiments), considerable indifference to vaccination against chicken pox, a raging debate about vaccinating girls for human papilloma virus, and fairly tepid compliance with recommendations for annual vaccination against the prevalent strain of influenza.

As the diseases we vaccinate against become less menacing, many citizens who have no hang-ups about vaccination per se just can't be bothered with the minor irritation and inconvenience. On the other hand, many people in modern societies are willing to go to considerable lengths to try to protect themselves against fairly unlikely contingencies. In the age of seat belts and airbags and children's car seats and helmets and life jackets, we no longer accept the levels of risk our parents took for granted.

Thus the stage was set for this year's public-health paradox: that the surprise appearance of an unusually mild strain of influenza is causing a state of immoderate alarm that is hugely disproportionate by past standards. To some extent it's been stirred up by journalists and public-health officials predisposed to attracting attention by trading in worst-case scenarios. More charitably, it has proved extremely difficult to strike a balance in explaining the threats posed by a new strain of virus and the strains that even a small proportion of severe cases might cause for an already over-burdened health-care system. It's a very serious situation but there's no need for alarm. we are told. If you aren't vaccinated you're not at serious risk but it's socially irresponsible not to get vaccinated. The subtleties get lost in the verbal shuffling.

Even in the old days of great crusades against terrible diseases, public-health officers could not take their authority for granted. In free societies, people make up their own minds, even on medical subjects. Conscientious objection is always an option. And when experts appear to be wrong or even confused in risk-assessment, especially if they're found to have been wrongly crying wolf, they can unintentionally undermine public confidence. Sometimes I think that our public-health experts could benefit from some of the more intelligent advice prescribed by professional media spin doctors.

THE THREE CAMPS

You would not know it from our public discourse, but Canadians, like the people of most prosperous countries, are among the healthiest humans in all of history. We live longer, our bodies are stronger, our physical capacities are greater than those of any peoples who came before us. Modern affluence, modern nutrition and modern medicine including the vaccines that have been so effective in protecting us from the really dreaded diseases have done wonderful things for us. No wonder most of us are glad to live at this time in history, rather than in any era of the plague-ridden past.

When it comes to thinking about our health, we seem to divide into three camps. At one extreme are the fearful, those predisposed to see health as infinitely fragile and constantly menaced by terrible threats killer viruses in the eyes of some, killer vaccines in the view of others. It's the anxious and the obsessive who are the loudest voices in the media, clog clinics and hospitals, fulminate against demonic vaccinators and incompetent doctors, and try to weave suffocating protective cocoons around themselves and their families.

At the other extreme are the indifferent and irresponsible, who take their good health, their apparent immunity to disease and disease-talk for granted, and just tune out. We're all right, Jack. Not our problem. Cough, cough.

Most Canadians probably fall into a middle group. They're sensible people who don't like being sick, care for each other and take reasonable precautions to protect themselves and others. They have a sense of their good fortune in living in the 21st century and a sense of proportion about their priorities. When our health is at risk, we pay attention to our doctors and take our medicines and our vaccines. Then we get on with the important things of life, which centuries of progress and dedicated public health professionals have made it possible for us to enjoy. We go to concerts and movies and schools and churches and Tim's and we shake hands and kiss and hug one another without giving disease a second thought.

Life's too short to be spent thinking about sickness.

BACKGROUND

The H1N1 flu virus is a new strain of pandemic influenza which is different than the seasonal flu. People have no natural immunity to protect against this virus. The H1N1 flu virus emerged in April 2009 and surveillance of its spread shows that it is affecting more young and healthy people than the regular seasonal flu, which normally affects seniors and young children. People with underlying medical conditions and pregnant women may be at a greater risk for severe illness.

WHAT IS IT?

The H1N1 flu virus - also known as human swine influenza - is a respiratory illness that affects the nose, throat and lungs. This virus usually affects pigs, but has been transferred to humans.

The H1N1 flu virus has caused primarily mild illness in Canada, but Canadians need to be prepared to respond to a potentially more severe form of the virus that may emerge this fall.

HOW IS IT SPREAD?

The H1N1 flu virus is contagious and is spread the same way as regular seasonal influenza. This happens when an infected person coughs or sneezes and their germs enter the nose, eyes, or throat of another person. The germs can also rest on hard surfaces like counters and doorknobs, and can be picked up on hands and transmitted to the respiratory system when someone touches their mouth and/or nose. It is not possible to catch it by eating pork or pork products or through blood transfusions.

CONTAGIOUSNESS

More research is being done on how long a person can be infectious (be able to spread the virus to others), but it is believed that this period is for one day before the onset of symptoms and continues for approximately seven days after symptoms have started. The time it takes between being infected and experiencing symptoms is between two and seven days.

Prevention

- Wash hands often

- Keep common surfaces disinfected

- Cough and sneeze into your arm, instead of your hand

- If you are sick, stay home until your symptoms are gone and you feel well enough to participate in all activities

- If you get flu-like symptoms and are pregnant, have underlying health problems or if your symptoms get worse, contact your health care provider.

TREATMENT

If you get flu-like symptoms and are pregnant or have underlying health problems contact your health-care provider.

If you get flu-like symptoms and are otherwise healthy, you should stay home to recover. If your symptoms worsen or you experience difficulty breathing or serious shortness of breath, it is important to seek medical attention.

Antivirals are drugs used for the early treatment of influenza. If taken shortly after getting sick (within 48 hours), they can reduce influenza symptoms, shorten the length of illness and potentially reduce the serious complications of influenza. Antivirals do not prevent you from getting sick.

Canada has a National Antiviral Stockpile of 55 million doses of two antiviral drugs -- Tamiflu and Relenza. Both are effective in treating H1N1 flu virus. This stockpile is enough for all Canadians who will need and want treatment.

Antivirals are recommended for the treatment of moderate to severe illness, and for people at risk of severe disease. Your doctor will decide if treatment is right for you.

VACCINATION

Influenza vaccines (also called flu shots) help you to prevent getting sick by introducing your body to a weakened or dead version of the virus to teach your body to build immunity to it.
